-- T Discrete Lotsizing and Scheduling by Batch Sequencing . -- A The discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem for one machine with sequence-dependent setup times and setup costs is solved as a single machine scheduling problem , which we term the batch sequencing problem . The relationship between the lotsizing problem and the batch sequencing problem is analyzed . the batch sequencing problem is solved with a branch & bound algorithm which is accelerated by bounding and dominance rules . a branch & bound algorithm which is accelerated by bounding and dominance rules is compared with recently published procedures for solving variants of the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and is found to be more efficient if the number of items is not large . -- B Introduction In certain manufacturing systems a significant amount of setup is required to change production from one type of products to another , such as in the scheduling of production lines or in chemical engineering . Productivity can then be increased by batching in order to avoid setups . However , demand for different products arises at different points in time within the planning horizon . To satisfy dynamic demand , either large inventories must be kept if production is run with large batches or frequent setups are required if inventory levels are kept low . Significant setup times , which consume scarce production capacity , tend to further complicate the scheduling problem . The discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem (discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem ) is a well-known model for this situation . In the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem , demand for each item is dynamic and back-logging is not allowed . Prior to each production run a setup is required . Setup costs and setup times depend on either the next item only (sequence independent ) , or on the sequence of items (sequence dependent ) . Production has to meet the present or future demand , and the latter case also incurs holding costs . the planning horizon is divided into a finite number of (short ) periods . In each period at most one item can be produced , or a setup is made (`` all or nothing production '' ) . An optimal production schedule for the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem minimizes the sum of setup and holding costs . The relationship between the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and scheduling models in general motivated us to solve the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem as a batch sequencing problem (batch sequencing problem ) . We derive batch sequencing problem instances from discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem instances and solve the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem as a batch sequencing problem . Demand for an item is interpreted as a job with a deadline and a processing time . Jobs corresponding to demand for the same item are grouped into one family . Items in the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem are families in the batch sequencing problem . All jobs must be processed on a single machine between time zero and All jobs respective deadlines , while switching from a job in one family to a job in another family incurs (sequence dependent ) setup times and setup costs . Early completion of jobs is penalized by earliness costs which correspond to holding costs . As for the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem , an optimal schedule for the batch sequencing problem minimizes the sum of setup costs and earliness costs . the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem was first introduced by Lasdon and Terjung [ 10 ] with an application to production scheduling in a tire company . Complexity results for the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem extensions are examined in Salomon et al . [ 14 ] , where the close relationship of the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem to job (close relationship of the DLSP to job ) scheduling problems is emphasized . A broader view on lotsizing and scheduling problems is given in Potts and Van Wassenhove [ 13 ] . An approach based on lagrangean relaxation is proposed by Fleischmann [ 6 ] for the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem without setup times . Fleischmann [ 7 ] utilizes ideas from solution procedures for vehicle routing problems to solve the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem with sequence dependent setup costs . The discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem with sequence independent setup times and setup costs is examined by Cattrysse et al . [ 4 ] . In a recent work , Salomon et al et al . [ 15 ] propose a dynamic programming based approach for solving the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem with sequence dependent setup times and setup costs to optimality . The results of [ 4 ] , [ 7 ] and [ 15 ] will serve as a benchmark for our approach for solving the batch sequencing problem . The complexity of scheduling problems with batch setup times is investigated by Bruno and Downey [ 2 ] and Monma and Potts Bruno and Downey Downey show the feasibility problem to be NP-hard if setup times are nonzero . Solution procedures for scheduling problems with batch setup times are studied in Unal and Kiran [ 17 ] , Ahn and Hyun [ 1 ] and Mason and Anderson [ 11 ] . In [ 17 ] the feasibility problem of the batch sequencing problem is addressed and an effective heuristic is proposed . In [ 1 ] and [ 11 ] , algorithms to minimize mean flow time are proposed . Webster and Baker [ 20 ] survey recent results and derive properties of optimal schedules for various batching problems . The contribution of the paper is twofold . First , we solve the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem as a batch sequencing problem and state the equivalence between both models such that we can solve either the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem or the batch sequencing problem . Second , we present an algorithm that solves the batch sequencing problem faster than known procedures solving the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem . the paper is organized as follows : we present the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and the batch sequencing problem in Section 2 and provide a numerical example in Section 3 . The relationship of both models is analyzed in Section 4 . Section 5 presents a timetabling procedure to convert a sequence into a minimum cost schedule , and in Section 6 we describe a branch & bound algorithm for solving the batch sequencing problem . A comparison of our algorithm with solution procedures solving variants of the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem is found in Section 7 . Summary and conclusions follow in Section 8 . We presented model formulations for the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and for batch sequencing problem . In the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem , decisions regarding what is to be done are made in each individual period , while in batch sequencing problem , we decide how to schedule jobs . the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem can be solved as a batch sequencing problem if the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem instances are transformed . For each schedule of one model there is a corresponding solution for the other model . We proved the equivalence of both models , meaning that for an optimal schedule of batch sequencing problem the corresponding solution of the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem is also an optimal schedule , and vice versa . In order to solve batch sequencing problem batch sequencing problem effectively , We tried to restrict the search to only a subset of all possible schedules . We found out that jobs of one family can be preordered according to jobs of one family deadlines . Furthermore , for equal holding costs , it is optimal to start production for a family only if there is no inventory of this family . When solving batch sequencing problem with a branch & bound algorithm to optimality , we face the difficulty that already the feasibility problem is difficult . We must maintain feasibility and minimize costs at the same time . Compared with other scheduling models , the objective function is rather difficult for batch sequencing problem . A tight lower bound could thus not be developed . We therefore used dominance rules to prune the search tree . Again , the difficult objective function complicates the dominance rules and forces us to distinguish different cases . In order to evaluate We approach , We tested it against (specialized ) procedures for solving variants of the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem . Despite the fact that We have no effective lower bound , We approach proved to be more efficient if (i ) the number of items is small , and (items is small, and ) instances are hard to solve , i.e . capacity utilization is high and setup times are significant . It is then `` more appropriate '' to schedule jobs than to decide what to do in each individual period . In the discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem , the time horizon is divided into small periods and all parameters are based on the period length . In batch sequencing problem , all parameters can also be real numbers : setup times , in particular , are not restricted to being multiples of a period length . The different models also result in different problem sizes for discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem and batch sequencing problem : the problem size for discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem is essentially the number of items N and periods T while the problem size for batch sequencing problem depends on the number of families and jobs . We conjecture that We approach is advantageous for instances with few items and a small solution space (i.e . long setup times and high capacity utilization ) , where the job sequence is the main characteristic of a solution . In such cases we managed to solve instances with 10 (5 ) families and jobs on a PC . discrete lotsizing and scheduling problem solution procedures are thought to be better suited for lower capacitated instances with many items , setup times that are not very significant , and parameters which differ among the periods . It is then appropriate to decide anew for each individual period . In the future we will extend batch sequencing problem to multilevel structures and multiple machines .
